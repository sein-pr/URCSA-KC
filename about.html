History and Leadership of the Church

History of the Church

In the second century, the church was already divided into the Eastern and Western Church. Since then, there have always been people in the church unhappy about certain developments.

Our church originates from a movement called "Protestant," as great scholars protested against the Roman Empire's system and practices. Key figures like Martin Luther and John Calvin significantly impacted the birth of our church.

Martin Luther

Luther opposed the indulgence system and questioned how a person could be certain of God’s grace. He famously nailed his 95 theses to the church door in Wittenberg. He also helped publish a Greek translation of the Bible and wrote many books to explain its meaning.

John Calvin

Calvin protested in Switzerland and France, advocating for a Reformed church system based on Christian education from the Bible. He wanted more lay participation and encouraged personal Bible reading.

Over time, figures like Zwingli and Beza contributed to the split of the Roman Catholic Church and the establishment of various Protestant churches in Europe.

In the 1490s, missionaries began arriving in Africa, and by the 1730s, Protestant churches like the Lutheran and Reformed churches reached South Africa. Our church, the Uniting Reformed in Southern Africa, originated there.

Initially named the Evangelical Reformed Church in Africa (EGKA), it changed its name post-independence from apartheid to the current name, reflecting its inclusive mission.

Leadership

The Church's Hierarchy Structure

The hierarchy is structured as follows:

  1. One General Synod Commission (S.A)
  2. Seven Regional Synod Commissions (6 in S.A, 1 in Namibia)
  3. Presbyteries Commissions (various in S.A; 5 in Namibia)
  4. Congregations (about 24 in Namibia)
  5. Church Council (governing body of each Congregation)
  6. Church members (the core of the Church’s existence)

Note: Congregations can establish Wards that may become independent if self-sustaining.

Church Order
